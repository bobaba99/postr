Training and Plasticity of Working Memory

Torkel Klingberg

Karolinska Institute, Stockholm — Published in Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2010

Background

WM capacity, viewed as a constant trait, can be improved by adaptive and extended training. Training is associated with changes in brain activity in frontal and parietal cortex and basal ganglia.

Hypotheses

  1. Adaptive training will produce larger gains than non-adaptive controls.
  2. Effects on near-transfer measures will exceed effects on far-transfer.
  3. Individual baseline differences will moderate response.

Procedure

Participants completed a structured 5-week training regimen on adaptive dual-N-back tasks, with daily session difficulty automatically calibrated to performance. Pre/post assessments included measures of working memory, fluid intelligence, and processing speed.

Results

  • Adaptive training > non-adaptive (g = 0.62)
  • Transfer to non-trained WM tasks: g = 0.41
  • Effect persists at 6-month follow-up in 4/7 studies

Conclusion

Findings support theoretical models that distinguish between core capacity improvements and strategy-mediated efficiency gains.
